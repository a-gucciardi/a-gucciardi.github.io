Graph representation learning, limits ?

Networks and graphs

Networks are universal descriptors of interacting elements, and it is something that our brains can understand. If it can be represented as a graph, then we get a topological and visual understanding of the system of interactions. It can represent anything and is used in all possible fields, but this post will focus on biomedical networks, a focus of my current research work.

Biomedical networks

Even within this category of networks, many different types have to be considered. Depending on the type of information encoded in a biological network, the interactions between 2 entities can differ, giving a different meaning to what an edge is. Similarly for the nodes, which can be molecular, chemical, tissue, population-scale, social, or just information entities. The interpretation of an edge depends on the context, but interactions are observed between comparable units. Network Biology and medicine have identified and can now use a range of principles to govern networks structures.

We can distinguish 4 main categories of networks structures: molecular phenotypes, biological roles, disease, and global health.

Beyond

Given these network types - and their different scales -, comes a thought. I wanted to focus on biology and that’s an important and large focus possible, but can’t we see further? Is it not just a part of what’s possible? And just like a complete network of what our body interactions are, can we abstract a network between more than 1 person? Of course, but can we include subgraphs into larger graphs, with different node types. I mean if it can somehow make sense and be abstracted to include molecular level graphs into tissue levels graphs into whole-body graphs, then we can model whole entities and their interactions. As written later in this blog post, even if the entire abstraction is not known we can learn to extract it given several examples and cases. In a sense, this creates a baseline. It can be a baseline of a normal human body graph, it can be a baseline of a human group, it can be a baseline of a whole country, or even -why not- the whole world? This of course requires different levels of abstraction with a finite set of nodes types possible. But how far can we go in the representation of base bricks of the whole thing I am trying to grasp here? Even if it is not precise enough, it then seems possible to extract and predict information that we can’t just see or grasp, just like when we train graph with representation learning the network gives us new insights. The application possibilities are insane. The idea of an understanding of our understandings, hidden but attainable, is chilling. > Even if we are not there yet, there must be glimpses, crumbs of a prototype for such a broad model.

Representation learning – Draft

Or how to use deep learning on graphs. How can we abstract graphs, even the most complex topological structures composed of the nodes and interactions that we know? In contrast classical deep learning uses summary data, ordered in one (or more) fixed dimension(s). But the network representations implies diverse structural properties and numerous interactions, which is vital information for our systems. Luckily for us graph representation learning exists, along with a large range of methods, which extract information from the interactions and use it to train a model. Representation learning techniques learn by transforming network structures into low-dimensional representations, using nonlinear dimensionality reduction. From this approach, 3 possible learning task on graphs emerge :

  • Node | link | property prediction: the goal is to learn transformed versions (ie. representations) of the elements from a given graph type : entire graphs, subgraphs, nodes or edges. A representation reflects the initial graph topology and allows operations without losing the important information (we wish). In a process similar to classical ml, representations can be used as datasets to train a model, to then predict the property of graph elements : tasks include graph classification, link (edge) prediction or node prediction.
  • Latent graph learning: concerns problem where graphs are partially known (typical in gene regulatory networks). The goal is to deduct the graph from the date, effectively inferring the network. The task can be narrowed down to a specific application, and optimise predictions from it. The graph can be the task, as an interpretation goal.
  • Graph generation: generate a graph likely to have a desired property (or set of properties). The model is trained thanks to graphs with such a property, tasking it to learn a lattent representation. The learned distribution is then used to optimise a new graph with the desired properties.
